    VII. Collective Thresholds: Beyond the Individual

    Liminalism, while deeply concerned with individual experience, offers a powerful lens through which to understand and navigate the dynamics of collectives – teams, organizations, communities, and even societies. Like individuals, these entities exist in states of transition, grapple with internal multiplicity, construct shared narratives, and mediate between their internal identity and the external environment. They are, in essence, collective thresholds.

    Just as individuals thrive by tending their thresholds, collectives flourish when their shared boundaries are navigated with care.

    Communities and Organizations as Thresholds

    Collectives function as dynamic, semi-permeable boundaries mediating between their members' internal lives and the shared external world. They have membership boundaries (who is in/out) and cultural boundaries (shared norms, values). Like the individual self, they must be flexible enough to adapt and integrate external changes or new members (becoming porous) while resilient enough to maintain a coherent identity and purpose (standing firm). Tending the collective threshold involves managing the flow of information, influence, and members across these boundaries.

    Collective Multiplicity and Narrative

    A collective is not a single unified mind, but a complex interplay of diverse individuals, departments, teams, subcultures, and often conflicting interests – a collective "multitude." The shared identity, mission, or culture of the collective is a form of collective narrative, a "useful fiction" that provides coherence, continuity, and a sense of shared purpose amidst internal diversity. Leaders and active members are key authors and editors of this ongoing story. Recognizing this multiplicity means valuing diverse perspectives and understanding that internal disagreements are not necessarily failures, but inherent aspects of a complex system.

    Collective Attentionality

    Just as individuals must manage their finite attention, collectives possess a limited capacity for shared focus and energy. Collective attentionality is the deliberate practice of a group choosing where to direct its shared awareness, resources, and efforts. What does the organization collectively notice (market signals, employee feedback, societal changes)? What does it focus on (strategic priorities, specific problems)? What does it witness(integrate lessons learned from projects, crises, or successes into its collective memory and strategy)? Effective collective attentionality is crucial for adaptation, learning, and achieving shared goals.

    Co-Creating Collective Meaning

    The shared purpose, values, and significance of a collective are not dictated from above or discovered as pre-existing entities. Collective meaning is co-created through the ongoing "conversation" among members, leadership, stakeholders, and in interaction with the external environment. Meetings, dialogues, shared experiences, and collaborative projects are all sites where collective meaning is forged and refined. Engaging the "gaps" between different internal groups or between the collective and its external context is essential for rich, dynamic meaning-making.

    Engaging Collective Gaps and Transitions

    Organizations and communities constantly navigate transitions – restructuring, leadership changes, market shifts, crises, growth, decline. These are collective liminal states, periods of heightened uncertainty but also potential transformation. Embracing the "gaps" within the collective (silos, cultural divides, misunderstandings) and between the collective and its environment (market disconnects, community tensions) means seeing these not just as problems to be eliminated, but as necessary spaces for dialogue, learning, and the emergence of new collective meaning and strategy.

    Embracing Collective Surprise and Incompleteness

    Collectives, like individuals, never have complete information or total control over their environment or even internal dynamics. Embracing this collective incompleteness and accepting surprise – market disruptions, unexpected challenges, unforeseen opportunities – is key to collective resilience and innovation. It requires fostering a culture that can "notice" the unexpected, "focus" on understanding it without immediately trying to force it into old models, and "witness" the lessons learned into collective narrative and practice. Collective humor can even be a way to navigate shared absurdity or unexpected failures.

    Collective Narrative Ethics

    The ethical implications extend to the collective level. Truthfulness in collective narrative means integrity in mission statements, transparent communication, and an honest accounting of history (revising the collective story without shame when necessary). Participation implies fostering environments where members feel seen, heard, and able to contribute their unique perspectives to the collective conversation and direction. Attentional discipline means aligning the collective focus with espoused values and ensuring that vulnerable signals from internal or external stakeholders are not ignored.

    Applying Liminalism to collectives encourages a view of organizations and communities not as static structures to be controlled, but as dynamic, complex, relational processes in a constant state of becoming. It highlights the importance of navigating transitions with intentionality, fostering open dialogue across internal and external boundaries, and consciously shaping the shared stories and attention that define collective identity and purpose.
