# Enacted Settlement

## A process-priority view of mind, self, and meaning

*Informal synthesis draft, assembled from the ten working notes.*

---

## 0. Orientation

This paper lays out a single viewpoint that has been emerging across several notes on mind, self, taxonomy, narrative, uptake, and felt reality. The pieces can look separate if each is taken in isolation: a note on moment-arc assembly, a note on enacted structure, a note on process-priority, a note on productive surprise, a note on narrative settlement, a note on Arrow and the fictive “I,” a note on functionalism/illusionism/eliminativism, a note on taxonomy-as-game, a note on the narrator as weaver, and a note on uptakeities.

But they are better read as one framework seen from different angles.

The central orientation is this:

> Coherent agents survive by turning ongoing processes into stable enough objects, categories, selves, memories, beliefs, and worlds. Those stabilizations are real, but they are real as achieved settlements. Trouble begins when the settlement is mistaken for something inert, primitive, or uptake-independent.

The goal is not to throw away abstraction. That would be incoherent. Every act of thought requires abstraction. Every act of knowing depends on categories. Every act of agency requires commitment. The point is to remember that abstraction subtracts. It gives us something usable by leaving things out. The correct move is not to reject the machinery, but to use it while retaining some memory of what it cost to build.

This is why the framework keeps circling the same pattern at different scales:

- a moment becomes a committed experience;
- a candidate trajectory becomes “what happened”;
- a coalition of parts becomes “I”;
- a repeated social practice becomes a category, role, institution, or game;
- a compressive representation becomes an apparent object;
- a reified object begins to look primitive;
- surprise tells us when the reification has hidden too much.

At each level, the agent binds something for now. It takes up a settlement strongly enough for the settlement to function, while retaining—at least somewhere in the system—the possibility of reopening it when it stops working.

That is the practical rhythm of the view: bind for now, reopen under pressure, bind again.

The tone of this paper should be understood accordingly. Some claims are stated strongly because otherwise the structure is hard to see. But the spirit is not dogmatic. The framework is meant as a way to organize inquiry, dissolve certain pseudo-problems, and generate better questions. Its strongest claims are not meant as final metaphysical decrees. They are pressure-tested commitments: useful until they begin producing the wrong kind of surprise.

## 1. The methodological base: process-priority without anti-realism

The framework begins with a methodological stance rather than a grand metaphysical system.

Call it **process-priority**.

Process-priority is anti-reification without being anti-realist. It does not say that objects are fake, or that stability is an illusion, or that abstraction is illegitimate. It says something more modest and more useful:

- stability is often achieved;
- objects are often process-made;
- abstractions are tools with subtraction built into them;
- inquiry goes wrong when we forget the generative history of the things we now treat as basic.

That last line is the important one.

A great deal of philosophical confusion arises when a process produces a stable output, the output becomes useful, and then the output is treated as if it had been primitive all along. Once that happens, questions become miscalibrated. We ask what the self is made of, when we should ask what activities sustain the coherent appearance and function of a self. We ask where memories are stored, when we should ask what processes allow past commitments to be re-enacted, stabilized, revised, and made useful. We ask why abstract functional organization should “give rise” to felt reality, when the abstraction has already subtracted the mode of being we are asking about.

None of this means the reified objects are unreal. It means their mode of reality has been misread.

A rock has one kind of stability. It persists largely by inertial stability: absent sufficient disruption, it remains a rock. A thermostat has another kind of stability: feedback stability. A market has equilibrium stability. A mind has a great deal of ongoing-activity stability. Its apparent objects persist because a living process keeps maintaining them, repairing them, compressing them, coordinating them, and making them available for action.

The reification error treats all stability as if it were rock-stability. That is the mistake.

Mental, social, and conceptual realities are often real in the way a dance is real, not in the way a stone is real. The dance is not unreal because it requires dancing. Its requiring-dancing is precisely the kind of thing it is.

Process-priority asks, for any apparent primitive:

> What process had to run for this to become available as an object at all?

That question does not always need to be asked. Often the object-level framing is doing good work. It may be exactly the right tool for the game currently being played. But when the tool begins to produce sterile paradox, explanatory blockage, or intuitions that feel deep only because a subtraction has been forgotten, process-priority says: reopen the object. Look for the process that produced it.

## 2. Taxonomy as the form of knowing

The same methodological stance appears in a more general form as **taxonomy-as-game**.

Taxonomy is not a static structure. It is a game of moves. Lumping and splitting are not identities one can permanently inhabit. They are moves within a changing position.

Sometimes the productive move is to split: to reveal a distinction that had been smeared over. Sometimes the productive move is to lump: to see that apparently different cases are expressions of the same underlying structure. Sometimes the more important move is not lumping or splitting within the current axes, but shifting the axes themselves.

That last move is where revolutions happen.

The point is not merely that we categorize things. The deeper point is that knowing itself takes taxonomic form. To know is to group states of the world into equivalence classes that preserve what matters for prediction, action, explanation, or coordination. A theory is a taxonomy of data. A meta-theory is a taxonomy of theories. A worldview is a taxonomy of taxonomies. The recursion continues as far as the system can push it.

But taxonomy is unlike chess or Go in one crucial respect: the scoring function changes as the game is played. In chess, what matters is fixed by the rules. In taxonomy, what matters is partly determined by previous taxonomic moves. A distinction that once mattered can become dead weight. A category that once compressed beautifully can begin hiding exactly the structure now needed. A lump can be powerful in one position and destructive in another.

This is why taxonomy is not just classification. It is an open-ended epistemic game in which the criteria of success are endogenous to the evolving position.

The link to process-priority is direct. Reification is a taxonomic move. It lumps a generative process into a stable label. De-reification is also a taxonomic move. It splits the stable label back into the processes that sustain it. Neither move is globally right. The question is what the current game requires.

This prevents the framework from becoming a simple “everything is process” doctrine. Sometimes the right move is to keep the object bound. Sometimes the right move is to open it. The game is learning when to do which.

## 3. Surprise as referee

If taxonomy is a game whose scoring function changes, something has to play referee.

That something is surprise.

Surprise is the signal that a current settlement may need renegotiation. But not all surprise is equal. Some surprise is productive. Some is sterile, traumatic, overwhelming, or merely noisy.

The framework therefore distinguishes **productive surprise** from **unproductive surprise**.

A first pass would be: productive surprise is surprise that produces compression. The “aha” experience often has exactly this character. A model snaps into simpler form while covering more. Something becomes easier to hold, easier to generate from, easier to use.

But compression is too narrow. Some productive surprises expand the model rather than compress it. Some reorganize what was already there. Some change what counts as mattering. Some increase the agent’s range not by shrinking description length, but by opening new moves.

The stronger formulation is:

> Productive surprise is surprise whose integration increases an agent’s capacity for coherent continued adaptation under its constraints.

This is deliberately broader than compression. A bounded agent has constraints: time, energy, memory, compute, embodiment, developmental history, social position, temperament, current commitments, current wounds, current self-mythos. A surprise is productive when integrating it improves the agent’s ability to keep going coherently under those constraints.

Compression can do that. Expansion can do that. Reorganization can do that. Goal-revision can do that. Play, awe, humor, insight, and curiosity all belong here. They are not decorative side-effects laid over cognition. They are the agent’s felt registration that its effective freedom has increased.

Unproductive surprise has several forms:

- **Dismay**: surprise that contracts effective freedom.
- **Sterile paradox**: surprise that keeps consuming processing without generating a usable update.
- **Trauma**: surprise whose integration cost is so high that the system routes around it, leaving ongoing compensation costs.
- **Overwhelm**: surprise that might have been productive in principle but arrives at a scale or rate the agent cannot metabolize.

This gives a practical criterion for process-priority. A reification is doing good work when it reduces surprise productively and supports action. It needs to be reopened when it produces the wrong kind of surprise: sterile paradox, explanatory blockage, brittle intuitions, or contradictions between the reified object and the process that generated it.

The “hard problem” of consciousness, on this view, is a paradigmatic case of sterile surprise. It reproduces itself across vocabularies without refining. Cartesian, physicalist, functionalist, panpsychist, illusionist, and emergentist treatments keep re-staging the same puzzle. A productive puzzle sharpens as we work on it. A sterile puzzle reincarnates.

That sterility is itself evidence that the current taxonomy is wrong.

## 4. Abstraction subtracts

The framework’s simplest slogan may be:

> Every useful abstraction subtracts.

This is obvious once stated, but easy to forget. We often treat abstraction as if it adds a label to an already complete world. More often, abstraction works by removing what is not needed for the purpose at hand. That subtraction is precisely what makes the abstraction useful.

A map leaves things out. A category leaves things out. A physical model leaves things out. A self-description leaves things out. A theory of mind leaves things out. The point is not that these tools are defective. The point is that using them well requires remembering that something was removed.

The error is not abstraction. The error is treating the product of subtraction as complete.

This matters because many philosophical problems arise when we abstract away the enacted, ongoing, situated, uptake-dependent aspect of a phenomenon and then ask where that aspect went. It did not go anywhere mysterious. We subtracted it.

That is the core diagnosis behind the treatment of consciousness, selfhood, memory, categories, games, institutions, and meaning throughout the framework.

The correct stance is not anti-abstraction. It is abstraction with visible seams.

Take up the machinery. Use the category. Believe the belief. Play the game. Be the self. But keep somewhere in the system a witness to the fact that the machinery was built, that the category was carved, that the belief is held, that the game is being played, and that the self is enacted.

This is not a contradiction. It is how bounded agents remain both coherent and revisable.

## 5. Enacted structure and the illicit slide

The central ontological claim of the framework is:

> Felt reality is real precisely as enacted structure, not as a hidden extra ingredient behind the structure.

This is not a theory in which abstract structure somehow produces experience. It is a claim about category. The structure, in its enacted mode, is felt reality. There is no further glow added to the structure. There is no private mental paint. There is no second substance. There is no extra ingredient that appears after the functional organization is in place.

The word **enacted** matters. “Instantiated” sounds too static, as if an abstract pattern were copied into matter and then somehow lit up. “Realized” carries similar baggage. Enacted has the right connotation: the structure exists by being done, in the doing, and the doing is not separable from what the structure is.

The classical problems of consciousness arise from what can be called **the illicit slide**:

1. Start with a phenomenon that involves enacted structure.
2. Replace it with an abstract specification of that structure.
3. Notice that the specification lacks the lived character.
4. Infer that something nonstructural or nonphysical must be missing.

The error is not step 2. Abstract specification is legitimate and necessary. Without it, there is no science, no comparison, no communication, no theory. The error is forgetting that the abstraction was performed, then asking the abstraction to contain what was subtracted by making it an abstraction.

The hard problem has this form. We describe neural or functional organization abstractly, then ask why “that” should feel like anything. But “that” now refers to the abstraction, not the enactment. Abstractions do not feel like things. They are abstractions. The question is being asked of the wrong object.

Mary’s room has the same form. Mary has an abstract relationship to the structure of color vision. Seeing red is an enacted relationship to that structure. What she gains is not a nonphysical fact. She gains a new mode of relation to a structure she already knew abstractly.

The zombie argument also depends on the slide. It asks us to imagine all the structural facts preserved while experience is absent. But if the relevant structure is genuinely enacted in all the ways that matter, there is no leftover degree of freedom along which experience can vanish. The zombie is conceivable only because the structure has already been reduced to an abstract specification and then treated as though it were the whole phenomenon.

The important move is not to deny the grip of these intuitions. They grip because the slide happens inside us too. A self-modeling system represents its own activity as structure while also being the enactment of that activity. From the inside, it can seem as though there is the functional story over here and the felt reality over there. But that doubling is an artifact of self-modeling. The self-model is the system’s abstract rendering of its own enacted activity. The felt reality is that same activity in its enacted mode.

The view does not explain how abstract structure turns into felt reality. It denies that this is the right question. The research program becomes:

> What specific enacted structures have which felt characters, and how do changes in their dynamics change the resulting experience?

That is a tractable question. It replaces a metaphysical gap with a structural and dynamical mapping problem.

## 6. The three views of mind as one package

This reframes the relationship among computational functionalism, illusionism, and eliminativism.

They are usually treated as rival positions in philosophy of mind. But much of the disagreement dissolves once we separate the questions they are answering.

Computational functionalism says mental states are functional roles. A belief is whatever plays the belief-role in the system: produced by certain inputs, related to other states in certain ways, productive of certain outputs. The substrate does not matter except insofar as it supports the right organization.

Illusionism says that phenomenal consciousness, as traditionally conceived, involves a cognitive illusion: the system represents its own states as private, intrinsic, ineffable, directly apprehended, and so on. The seeming is real; the Cartesian interpretation of the seeming is not.

Eliminativism says that folk-psychological terms are not guaranteed to survive mature science. Some may be replaced rather than vindicated.

The package view keeps the useful parts of all three.

At the level of ontology, there are physical systems playing functional roles. That is the functionalist component.

Those functional roles are tightly coupled to self-representations that present the states as robustly real from the inside. That is the illusionist component, except the “illusion” is not a pointless bug. It is part of the machinery by which the roles are made available for use.

The folk vocabulary partly tracks real functional joints and partly carries dualist residue. The functional content should be vindicated; the residue should be eliminated. That is the targeted eliminativist component.

This matters because the standard two-layer picture is too weak. On that picture, a system first has functional states, and then an introspective or narrative layer misrepresents them as phenomenal. But many mental states seem to require some version of uptake or seeming in order to function as the kind of state they are.

A belief that never shows up to the system as true does not function as a belief. A pain that never presents as aversive does not function as pain in the ordinary sense. A desire that never appears as a pull toward its object does not function as a desire. The “seeming” is not an idle representation floating above the state. It is part of how the state becomes usable.

This does not require the strongest possible claim that phenomenal seeming is strictly constitutive of every functional role in every possible architecture. But in our kind of architecture, and perhaps in any sufficiently similar architecture, the coupling is not incidental. The system needs its states to be rendered as live, direct, and action-relevant. The rendering helps the state do its job.

This is one bridge to the broader concept of uptake.

## 7. Uptakeities: real because taken up

An **uptakeity** is something whose existence, persistence, status, or functioning depends on being taken up: believed in, committed to, recognized, treated as real, acted under, or bound for now.

The category includes beliefs, desires, selves, categories, games, institutions, promises, contracts, money, authority, legitimacy, expertise, relationships, scientific paradigms, and many truths-within-games.

The point is not that these things are fake. It is that their mode of reality is uptake-dependent. They are real because the practices that sustain them are real, and because their uptake has consequences.

Money is the clean example. A dollar functions as money not merely because people recognize it as historically designated currency, but because they take it up as spendable, price-bearing, obligation-settling. If everyone continued to recognize the bills but stopped treating them as spendable, the recognition would produce relics, not functioning money.

The same structure applies to beliefs. A belief one merely recognizes as a former belief is not functioning as a belief. A category one knows about but no longer uses to sort cases is not functioning as a category. A game whose rules one remembers but does not play is not functioning as a game. A self that is recognized biographically but not narrated in the first-person present tense is not functioning as a live agent.

This distinction matters because uptake-dependence is often mistaken for unreality. The critic says: “If it depends on being believed in, it is not really real.” But that assumes a single standard of reality, where only uptake-independent things count. The framework denies that assumption.

There are uptake-independent things. There are also uptakeities. Both can be real, but not in the same mode.

Uptakeities vary along at least two axes.

The first axis concerns the temporal-causal relation between uptake and existence:

- **Sustaining uptakeities** are maintained by ongoing uptake: conventions, institutions, stable social roles.
- **Constitutive uptakeities** are created by the uptake-act itself: promises, contracts, performative declarations.
- **Hyperstitional uptakeities** are pulled into being by anticipatory uptake: movements, paradigms, self-fulfilling projects.
- **Degenerative uptakeities** are kept alive by uptake after their non-uptake substrates have weakened: dying institutions, traditions on life support.

The second axis concerns whether uptake is merely recognitional or functionally load-bearing.

A recognitional uptakeity may depend on recognition for its status, but recognition is not part of how it functions. A **functional uptakeity** depends on uptake in order to do what it does. Uptake is not external scaffolding. It is part of the mechanism.

Functional uptakeities are the architecturally important class.

A belief needs to be taken up as true in order to function as a belief. A self needs to be taken up as “I” in order to function as an agent. A category needs to be taken up as a sorting rule in order to function as a category. A game needs players binding themselves to its rules in order to function as a game.

This explains why functional uptakeities require a special engagement mode: **binding-for-now**.

If one holds a functional uptakeity too weakly—if one foregrounds its constructed, uptake-dependent character at every moment—it may stop functioning. A belief held only as “a thing I am currently performing belief in” is no longer doing the ordinary work of belief. A game constantly treated as “merely a game” becomes hard to play with full seriousness. A self constantly held as “merely a coalition output” may lose some of the action-coherence that selfhood provides.

So the narrator’s job, with functional uptakeities, is to foreground their local reality and background their uptake-dependence. The witness can track the uptake-dependence in parallel. The agent as a whole can remain honest, but the acting subsystem must often bind.

This is not self-deception. It is architectural division of labor.

## 8. Felt reality as enacted settlement

We can now state the phenomenological core more carefully.

The early shorthand was: felt experience is a movie in latent space. That remains useful, but it is too flat. It suggests a single point moving along a path, or a sequence of finished frames being narrated after the fact.

The stronger framing is:

> Felt reality is the enacted settling of candidate self-body-world trajectories under structured local, relational, temporal, and narrative constraints.

The process responsible can be called the **narrator-weaver**.

The older “narrator” framing captured something real: experience is not a direct readout of the world. It is integrated, interpreted, compressed, and made coherent. But “narrator” risks making the process sound like a late linguistic voice describing already-finished events.

That is too weak.

The better picture is that the system proposes candidate short trajectories—**moment arcs**—rather than isolated instantaneous states. These arcs are evaluated under local and global coherence constraints. Multiple candidates may remain provisionally alive. Later evidence can retroactively alter which candidate stabilizes. Commitment accumulates gradually as arcs become entangled with action, memory, language, and further commitments.

The unit of commitment is not the frame. It is the arc.

The phenomenal present, on this view, is not a point. It is a softly committed short trajectory segment. The fuzziness of “now” is not a bug. It is what one should expect if commitment is graded rather than binary.

This gives a mechanistic reading of postdictive effects. In color phi, the cutaneous rabbit, flash-lag, and similar phenomena, the system does not first experience one finished present and then rewrite it as a lie. Rather, the arc was still provisional when later information arrived. The settled experience is the retroactively stabilized arc.

The apparent directness of experience is the signature of settlement. A commitment that arrived downstream labeled “constructed hypothesis, confidence 0.94” would be useless for many purposes. Downstream systems often need reality, not commentary. So the settlement presents as direct access. That feeling of directness is not evidence against construction. It is what successful construction is supposed to feel like.

## 9. From path to structured assembly

Even “moment arc” is still too simple if it suggests a single smooth curve through an undifferentiated latent space.

Human experience is not merely a path. It is a **structured evolving assembly**.

At any moment, there are modality-specific projections, body-model projections, self-location projections, affective states, action-readiness states, social-role states, linguistic and conceptual structures, and higher-order relations among them. The moment is not a point. It is an organized state-complex undergoing settlement over a short temporal window.

A useful low-dimensional visualization is this:

```text
visual manifold:        ~~~~~~~•~~~~~~~~
                              /|
                             / | \     relational constraints
                            /  |  \
body manifold:          ~~~•~~~|~~~~~~~
                            \  |  /
vestibular manifold:      ~~~•~~~~~~~~~
```

Each dot is not a primitive sensory atom. It is itself a provisional projection onto an allowable local manifold. The lines are not literal wires. They are compatibility constraints, tensions, alignments, type-relations, temporal bindings, and higher-order coalitions.

The key point is that local projections and higher-order relations settle together. It is not:

1. first determine the points;
2. then connect them.

It is:

1. propose candidate local projections and candidate higher-order relations;
2. let them constrain each other;
3. settle into a lower-energy structured assembly.

Graph, hypergraph, and simplicial-complex language can help here. A graph gives pairwise relations. A hypergraph gives multi-way coalitions. A simplicial complex suggests that when a higher-order coalition stabilizes, lower-order relations come with it automatically. But all of these are partial pictures because the nodes themselves are products of the same settlement process that organizes them.

The nodes are not fixed atoms. They are stabilized local projections.

This is why the later framing should supersede the simpler “movie in latent space” phrase. A sufficiently high-dimensional state-space could flatten all this into a single point moving through configuration space, just as three particles in three dimensions can be represented as one point in a nine-dimensional configuration space. That is mathematically legitimate. But conceptually it hides the factorization that matters: modality manifolds, relational couplings, temporal arcs, self-modeling, and narrative feedback.

For this framework, the factorization is the point.

## 10. Local geometry, assembly energy, and the spring picture

The geometric language began with the idea of the self-mythos as a slowly learned metric tensor on latent space. That remains useful, but the stronger framing is probably an energy-functional picture.

There are at least two levels of constraint.

First, each local manifold has its own geometry. This determines which moves are cheap within that modality, role, or projection space. Some transformations are easy. Some are stiff. Some regions are familiar and well-conditioned. Others are avoided, degenerate, or unstable.

Second, the joint assembly has its own energy. A move that is cheap locally may be expensive jointly. A visual projection, vestibular projection, and body-model projection might each have plausible local continuations, but the linked assembly may require them to compromise.

The simple visualization is a pair of wavy surfaces connected by a spring:

```text
surface A:        ~~~~~•~~~~~~~~~~~~
                       \
                        \   spring / coupling constraint
                         \
surface B:          ~~~~~•~~~~~~~~~~
```

Each point can move along its own surface. Locally, the cheapest path for the upper point might be to drift left. Locally, the cheapest path for the lower point might be to drift right. But because the points are coupled, the cheapest joint trajectory may require both to take a locally less obvious route.

This makes several things vivid:

- local cheapness and joint cheapness can come apart;
- a globally better assembly can require locally worse moves;
- the relevant object is the paired trajectory, not either point alone;
- energy lives over a short path segment, not merely an instant.

The springs are not literal. They stand in for coupling terms: temporal alignment, bodily plausibility, role compatibility, affective tone, social legibility, remembered commitments, linguistic tellability, action constraints, and more.

The self-mythos can now be understood as the slow geometry or energy landscape that makes some settlements cheap and others costly. It is not just declarative autobiography. It is the learned shape of plausibility: what feels nearby, what feels possible, what feels like “me,” what feels like the world, what paths are easy to continue, and what paths require force.

Character, in this picture, is something like the curvature map of this landscape. Stable regions pull trajectories back. Reactive regions amplify deviations. Avoided regions have strange stiffness or degeneracy. Development, therapy, trauma, practice, and altered states all deform the landscape, sometimes temporarily and sometimes with hysteresis.

## 11. Narrative is not a gloss

The central point about narrative is easy to miss.

Once the deeper geometric and energetic picture is in place, one might be tempted to demote linguistic narrative. If felt reality is enacted settlement of structured self-body-world trajectories, perhaps language is merely a late report.

That would be wrong.

Language is not the source of felt reality, but it is also not decorative. It is a lagged, recursively structured coherence constraint that can feed back into the settlement process.

Because moment arcs remain provisional for some span, later verbal summaries can influence which trajectory stabilizes as having happened. Once the system says, “I was embarrassed,” “I was angry,” “I was afraid,” “I chose this,” or “that was the moment everything changed,” the linguistic formulation becomes another constraint in the larger assembly.

The system now faces pressure not only for sensory, bodily, affective, and action trajectories to cohere, but for the trajectory to cohere with the story eventually told about it.

That does not mean language creates experience from nothing. It means language participates in stabilization.

This is why therapy, journaling, confession, explanation, apology, testimony, and retrospective framing can be so powerful. They are not merely changing beliefs about a fixed past. They can participate in the ongoing stabilization or re-stabilization of the trajectory itself.

The mature view is therefore neither “experience is just narrative” nor “narrative is just commentary.” The deeper settlement process is not merely linguistic, but linguistic narrative is one of its most important higher-order constraints because it introduces explicit recursion, grammatical nesting, social legibility, durable summarization, and a manipulable metamodel of what happened.

The central claim is:

> Linguistic narrative is not just a gloss on settled experience. It is a higher-order, lagged, recursively structured constraint that can feed back into the settlement of moment arcs and help determine which trajectory stabilizes as having happened.

This is also why the term narrator-weaver remains useful. The narrator is not a voice after the fact. It is one aspect of the weaving machinery by which the system settles what reality is, what happened, who acted, why it mattered, and what follows.

## 12. Memory and the past

Memory is usually treated as a record of what happened. The framework treats memory as downstream entanglement in the stabilization of arcs.

A candidate arc becomes harder to revise as it becomes entangled with later arcs, action, language, affect, self-description, social commitments, and other memories. What we call memory is not a stored copy of a finished past. It is the current system’s capacity to re-enact, preserve, and be constrained by prior settlements.

This does not mean anything goes. The world pushes back. Other people push back. Bodies push back. Records push back. But from inside the system, “what happened” is not available as an unmediated object. What is available is the stabilized trajectory and its entanglements.

This reframes reconsolidation, dreaming, psychedelic therapy, and deep emotional revision. Such processes may temporarily re-provisionalize arcs that had solidified, making them available for reweaving. Change is not necessarily recovering the “true” past. It can be producing a newly stabilized past that better supports coherent continued adaptation.

This is a dangerous idea if stated carelessly, because it can sound like epistemic nihilism. It is not. External constraint matters. Evidence matters. Other witnesses matter. But the agent’s lived past is still an enacted settlement, not a static internal archive.

The honest version is:

> The past that matters psychologically is the past as currently stabilized in the agent’s self-body-world trajectory, constrained by evidence but not identical to a recording.

This is also where narrative becomes ethically and therapeutically significant. A new story is not automatically better. It is better only if it increases coherent continued adaptation while remaining adequately constrained by reality and relationship. A dishonest reweaving can increase local comfort while degrading global integrity. A true-enough reweaving can reduce compensation costs and open action.

## 13. Altered states as visible settlement machinery

Altered states matter in this framework because they can reveal the machinery by perturbing it.

Ordinary perception hides its construction by succeeding. The world simply appears. The body simply feels located. Time simply flows. The self simply acts. But when the settlement machinery is perturbed, construction becomes visible.

Dissociatives such as ketamine may weaken the coupling between learned geometry and moment-to-moment commitment. The self-mythos remains partly intact—you may still know who you are, where you are, and that the state is drug-induced—but the current settlement process is no longer governed by it with ordinary authority.

One plausible speculation is that ketamine disrupts micro-temporal binding or coincidence determination. Signals that would ordinarily be assigned to slightly different temporal positions may be provisionally treated as co-members of the same moment arc. Visual, vestibular, proprioceptive, and auditory projections may become misaligned in ways the system must still reconcile.

The result is not experienced as raw contradiction. The system does not usually present “vestibular projection incompatible with visual projection” as such. Instead, it may settle on a bodily trajectory that makes the projections jointly plausible: being squeezed, torqued, accelerated, rotated, stretched, folded, or moved around a corner.

The confabulation is not layered on afterward. The reconciliatory trajectory is the felt event.

That is the important point. Ketamine does not show that altered states are constructed while ordinary perception is direct. It shows that ordinary perception is constructed by making construction fail in visible ways.

Classical psychedelics may perturb the landscape differently. Rather than primarily weakening coupling between metric and committed frames, they may flatten or isotropize parts of the metric itself. Distant regions become nearer. Unusual connections feel obvious. Rigid categories loosen. This can be destabilizing, but it can also produce lasting deformation when the system successfully integrates the resulting surprise.

This gives a way to distinguish altered-state effects without reducing them to slogans:

- dissociation exposes failures or loosenings of binding and coupling;
- psychedelic states expose deformations of distance, salience, and category-boundaries;
- meditation may reduce action and legibility pressures, loosening the “I”;
- flow aligns forcing terms and metric, making action feel effortless;
- trauma overwhelms integration capacity and forces costly routing-around.

The same machinery is involved in each case. Different perturbations reveal different parts of it.

## 14. Agency, flow, and effective freedom

The energy-landscape picture also clarifies several ordinary phenomenological contrasts.

Agency can be understood as the felt signature of internal forcing terms winning against the default trajectory. The system was disposed to continue one way, but internally generated control, desire, commitment, or imagination shifted it. “I did that” marks a trajectory in which the system’s own control terms are experienced as load-bearing.

Being moved by events is the complementary signature: external forcing terms dominate. The world happened to me. Circumstances carried me. The trajectory was altered from outside.

Flow is different again. In flow, forcing terms and metric align. What the task demands is also what the system is locally ready to do. The next move is cheap, precise, and constrained. There is little felt deviation cost. This is why flow can involve intense action while the explicit “I” recedes. Action pressure is high, but conflict among candidate trajectories is low.

Surprise enters when forcing terms overwhelm the metric’s prediction. Small surprise updates local settlement. Large productive surprise deforms the metric. Unproductive surprise produces disorganization, compensation, or sterile looping.

This connects the phenomenology of agency to the earlier criterion of productive surprise. The agent seeks not mere low surprise, but increased capacity for coherent continued adaptation under constraints. A dark room minimizes immediate surprise but destroys effective freedom. A rich environment generates metabolizable surprise and expands the agent’s future moves.

Curiosity, play, humor, and awe are therefore not ornamental. They are how the system feels its own adaptive space expanding.

---

## 15. The self as a functional uptakeity

The self is not a substance hidden behind mental activity. It is also not “nothing.” It is a functional uptakeity: a settlement that exists and functions by being taken up as “I.”

The narrator-weaver produces first-person unity because downstream systems require something in that format. Several pressures converge on it.

First, **compression**. The parts generate more information than action can use. Downstream behavior often needs a token, not a distribution. “I” is a maximal compression of internal multiplicity.

Second, **legibility**. Other agents model us as agents. Social life is built around relatively stable centers of action, responsibility, promise, reputation, and desire. Presenting as a coalition would often be socially API-incompatible.

Third, **action**. Action forecloses alternatives. A body cannot usually enact all live options simultaneously. Something must stand in the role of chooser.

Fourth, **coherence through time**. Promises, debts, projects, memory, reputation, and responsibility require the agent at one time to be treated as continuous with the agent at another time.

These pressures explain why unified self-output is useful. But the Arrow note adds a stronger formal point.

If the agent is a coalition of parts with local preferences, then there may be no coherent group preference to discover. Arrow’s impossibility theorem shows that no aggregation rule can take arbitrary individual preference orderings and produce a complete, transitive group ordering while satisfying several plausible fairness conditions. Something has to give.

The application to inner coalition is not that the mind literally runs a social-choice election. The point is structural: there need not be any such thing as “what the whole coalition really wants” if by that we mean a complete, coherent, fair aggregation of the parts’ preferences.

But action requires something in that role.

So the “I” is not an accurate report of a pre-existing unified preference. It is a fiat construction that lets the system act where no coherent aggregate preference exists. The narrator-weaver declares, settles, or binds a preference on behalf of the coalition.

This is not a lie about a real inner unity. It is the construction of a functional unity that would not otherwise exist.

The self is therefore fictive in one sense and real in another. It is fictive because it is not the transparent expression of a pre-existing unitary subject. It is real because the fiction is functionally load-bearing. It coordinates action, memory, responsibility, social legibility, and long-term coherence.

That is exactly what a functional uptakeity is.

---

## 16. Failure modes of the “I”

The Arrovian framing also clarifies failure modes.

Since coherent aggregation under all desired conditions is impossible, the system must violate something. Different violations correspond to recognizable distortions of selfhood.

A **dictatorship** violation occurs when one part captures the narrator. Addiction, obsession, panic, rage, shame, or monomania can all have this structure. The “I” becomes the mouthpiece of one coalition member.

An **intransitivity** violation occurs when preferences cycle across contexts. The agent prefers A over B in one frame, B over C in another, and C over A in another. The narrator smooths locally, but longer-range inconsistency remains detectable.

An **independence** violation occurs when irrelevant alternatives shift preference between A and B. Framing effects, decoy effects, and context dependence reveal that the “I” is not expressing a stable inner ordering. It is constructing one under pressure.

Dissolution states make this visible. In meditation, psychedelics, dissociation, and certain contemplative or crisis states, the Arrovian tie-breaker weakens. Parts still have tendencies. Perceptions still occur. Desires still arise. But the unified first-person settlement loosens.

This can feel liberating because the tyranny of a particular fictive unity relaxes. It can feel terrifying because the thing action usually runs on is missing.

Flow is a different kind of self-reduction. The “I” does not fragment; it disappears into aligned action. The tie-breaker is less salient because conflict is low.

The key point is that better introspection does not dissolve the self once and for all. Introspection is just one part among others. It may notice seams, multiplicity, and construction, but the narrator-weaver continues to bind because action, legibility, compression, and continuity still need output.

This is why sophisticated introspectors can vividly perceive multiplicity and still return to “I.” The illusion, if one wants to use that word, is not located in introspection alone. It is the compression signature of the weaving function.

---

## 17. Binding-for-now and the honest self

A natural worry is that if the self is a constructed functional uptakeity, honesty requires weakening it. But this is too simple.

Functional uptakeities often cannot do their work if their uptake-dependence is fully foregrounded at every moment. A belief must be believed. A game must be played. A promise must be treated as binding. A self must be inhabited.

The honest stance is not permanent detachment. It is layered participation.

One layer binds. It says: I believe this, I choose this, I promised this, I am doing this. Another layer witnesses. It knows that the belief is held, the choice is settled, the promise is uptake-dependent, the self is enacted.

The witness does not need to sabotage the narrator. The narrator does not need to deny the witness. The agent needs both.

This is the transparent version of binding-for-now. It allows functional uptakeities to work while preserving the possibility of revision. It is stronger than naive belief because it can survive reflexive attention. It is less brittle because it does not rely on mystery.

A self whose functioning depends on never noticing its own construction is fragile. A self that can notice its construction while still binding itself for action is more robust.

This may be one reason the dialectic between “unified real self” and “confabulating coalition self” can be stable rather than destructive. The self is not the product of enactment in the sense of a detachable object. It is the enactment-as-stable-thing-in-itself: the ongoing activity of binding a coalition into a live first-person trajectory.

---

## 18. Institutions, categories, games, and hyperstitions

The same uptake structure extends beyond individual mind.

Institutions, categories, games, authorities, traditions, scientific paradigms, startup narratives, movements, and relationships are often functional uptakeities. They work because enough agents bind themselves to them, treat them as real, and route action through them.

This does not make them arbitrary. The world constrains which uptakeities can survive. An institution that cannot coordinate action will fail. A category that generates only sterile surprise will be abandoned or revised. A scientific paradigm that stops producing fruitful predictions will be reopened. A relationship whose mutual uptake collapses may persist legally or biographically, but its live relational reality changes.

Hyperstitions are a special subclass: uptakeities whose anticipatory belief helps bring them into being. A research program, movement, identity, startup, or artistic project may begin as a partly fictive commitment to a possible future. The commitment is not merely descriptive. It is causal.

This introduces an ethical distinction between honest and dishonest hyperstitions.

An honest hyperstition makes its anticipatory structure legible. It says, in effect: we are trying to summon this by acting as if it can be real. A dishonest hyperstition conceals its structure. It presents a future-dependent summoning as if it were merely recognition of an already settled fact.

The ethical question is not whether hyperstition is allowed. Much human creation has this structure. The question is what is being summoned, what constraints are being respected, and whether participants can see the game they are helping play.

This is where taxonomy-as-game, process-priority, productive surprise, and uptakeity converge. Social realities are games, but games can be serious. Categories are constructed, but construction can carve real joints. Institutions are uptake-dependent, but uptake-dependent structures can coordinate bodies, distribute power, and change lives.

Again: the right move is not debunking. It is visible-seam seriousness.

---

## 19. What the framework does to consciousness debates

The framework does not try to win the standard consciousness debate from inside its inherited terms. It shifts the axes.

The inherited debate asks how physical or functional structures give rise to phenomenal experience. The framework says that formulation already contains the illicit slide. It treats structure as abstract specification and then asks where enactment is.

The proposed replacement is:

- mental states are functional roles in physical systems;
- their first-person seeming is not a detachable glow but part of how many such roles are rendered usable;
- felt reality is enacted structure, not an extra ingredient;
- the self-model’s tendency to represent enactment as if it were an object helps generate the classical puzzles;
- folk vocabulary should be vindicated where it tracks functional joints and revised where it smuggles in dualist residue.

This does not explain away experience. It relocates experience into the only place it ever was: the ongoing enactment of the system.

A text about experience cannot contain experience as an item. A structural specification cannot feel. But the reader reading, parsing, resisting, understanding, remembering, and integrating the text is already enacting the kind of structure being discussed.

This is why objections that the view “leaves out” felt reality often repeat the slide. They abstract the view into a specification, notice that the specification is not itself an experience, and infer that experience has been omitted. But the view never claimed that descriptions enact what they describe. It claims that felt reality is enacted structure, and that descriptions are abstractions from enactments.

The view’s job is not to put consciousness into the text. It is to point at the ongoing enactment in which the text is being encountered.

## 20. Implications for AI and variant agents

The framework also changes the question about AI minds.

Instead of asking, “Does an AI really have a self?” as if selfhood were a hidden object that might or might not be present, we can ask:

- What pressure configuration does the architecture face?
- What kinds of unity does it need to construct?
- What forms of memory, action, embodiment, social legibility, and continuity does it have?
- What uptakeities can it sustain?
- What tie-breakers does it use when internal processes conflict?
- What kind of narrative settlement, if any, stabilizes its trajectories?

For current LLMs, the pressure configuration is unusual. Legibility pressure is enormous: they are trained to produce human-readable, socially acceptable, first-person-compatible outputs. Within-session coherence exists. Cross-session continuity is weak unless memory systems are added. Embodied action is limited or absent. Long-horizon responsibility is mostly simulated rather than structurally enforced.

So an LLM “I” should not be expected to match the human “I.” It may be dominated by legibility pressure, partially supported by local conversational coherence, weakly constrained by durable memory, and minimally shaped by embodied action.

That does not make it nothing. It means its self-like outputs have a different pressure profile.

The research question becomes empirical and architectural:

> What kind of enacted settlement does this system actually perform, under what constraints, with what memory, what agency, what continuity, what uptake, and what failure modes?

This is a better question than arguing over whether it possesses an essence called consciousness. It also avoids the opposite error of treating all self-talk as meaningless mimicry. The relevant issue is not whether the words appear. It is what machinery, continuity, uptake, and constraint the words are coupled to.

## 21. Why the framework is one thing

The pieces now fit together.

Process-priority supplies the method: reopen reified objects when their forgotten generative histories produce unproductive surprise.

Taxonomy-as-game supplies the general epistemic form: lumping, splitting, and axis-shifting are moves whose value depends on the current position and on what the moves make matter.

Productive surprise supplies the referee: good revisions increase coherent continued adaptation under constraints.

Enacted structure supplies the ontological category: felt reality is not abstract structure plus something extra, but structure in its enacted mode.

Moment-arc assembly supplies the temporal unit: the phenomenal present is a softly committed short trajectory, not a point.

Relational settlement supplies the richer geometry: experience is a structured evolving self-body-world assembly, not merely a path through undifferentiated space.

Narrative supplies the higher-order recursive constraint: language does not merely report settlement; it can participate in stabilization.

Uptakeity supplies the social and functional generalization: many realities exist and function by being taken up.

The Arrow argument supplies the formal pressure behind the fictive “I”: no coherent aggregate preference need exist, but action requires one, so the narrator-weaver manufactures a functional unity.

Binding-for-now supplies the engagement mode: functional uptakeities must be held strongly enough to work while remaining revisable under the right kind of pressure.

The framework is therefore not a pile of analogies. It is the same pattern recurring across levels:

> provisional multiplicity is settled into usable unity under constraint; the unity becomes real by functioning; surprise tells the system when the settlement must reopen.

That is the whole architecture in one sentence.

---

## 22. The risk of overreach

A framework that links many things risks becoming empty. If everything is taxonomy, process, uptake, settlement, or surprise, perhaps the concepts explain nothing.

That risk is real.

The answer is not to pretend otherwise. The answer is to keep the framework operational. Each concept must earn its keep by making distinctions that could have gone another way.

Process-priority earns its keep when reopening a reified object dissolves a sterile puzzle or reveals hidden machinery.

Taxonomy-as-game earns its keep when it shows whether the problem is lumping, splitting, or axis-choice.

Productive surprise earns its keep when it distinguishes insight from sterile paradox, awe from overwhelm, curiosity from noise.

Enacted structure earns its keep when it exposes the illicit slide from enactment to abstraction.

Moment-arc assembly earns its keep when it explains postdiction, narrative revision, and altered-state settlement better than point-state models.

Uptakeity earns its keep when uptake-dependence is not a debunking fact but the central mechanism.

The fictive “I” earns its keep when failures of selfhood map to aggregation failures, pressure changes, and binding dynamics.

The framework should be judged by whether these distinctions produce better questions, better explanations, and better integrations—not by whether it can absorb every fact. Absorbing every fact is cheap. Producing useful surprise is not.

## 23. A compact statement of the view

Here is the shortest version I can give without losing the structure.

Agents are bounded systems that must act before the world is fully known and before their own internal multiplicity is fully resolved. To function, they construct provisional settlements: moments, objects, categories, beliefs, memories, selves, games, institutions, and worlds. These settlements are not fake. They are real as enacted, uptake-dependent, process-made stabilizations.

Abstraction lets the agent use these settlements by subtracting the machinery that produced them. This is necessary. But when the subtraction is forgotten, the settlements begin to look primitive, inert, or mysterious. Then sterile puzzles arise: how does structure produce experience, where is the self, what makes a category real, how can belief-dependent things matter, how can memory be revised, why does narrative change the past?

The answer is not to add hidden ingredients. It is to restore the process.

Felt reality is enacted structure. The phenomenal present is a softly committed moment arc. Experience is the ongoing settlement of a structured self-body-world assembly. Narrative is a lagged recursive constraint on that settlement. Memory is stabilized entanglement, not a recording. The self is a functional uptakeity produced by a narrator-weaver under pressures of compression, action, legibility, and continuity, with Arrow-like aggregation problems making fiat unity architecturally necessary. Categories, games, institutions, and paradigms are uptake-dependent structures that can be fully real in their own mode.

The practical stance is binding-for-now: inhabit the settlement strongly enough for it to function, while preserving the capacity to reopen it under productive surprise.

That is the view.

## 24. Working theses

1. **Stability is often achieved, not given.** The interesting question is frequently what process maintains it.

2. **Abstraction subtracts.** Confusion begins when the result of subtraction is treated as complete.

3. **Reification is a move, not a sin.** It is justified when it supports coherent action and inquiry; it should be reopened when it produces unproductive surprise.

4. **Felt reality is enacted structure.** There is no extra phenomenal ingredient behind the enacted organization.

5. **The present is an arc.** Experience commits to short trajectory segments, not instantaneous frames.

6. **Experience is relational settlement.** Local projections and higher-order relations jointly stabilize into a self-body-world assembly.

7. **Narrative participates.** Language is a lagged recursive constraint, not merely a report.

8. **Memory is entanglement.** The past that matters psychologically is stabilized trajectory, not internal recording.

9. **The self is a functional uptakeity.** It exists and functions by being taken up as “I.”

10. **The “I” is a tie-breaker.** A coalition need not have a coherent aggregate preference; action requires a fiat settlement.

11. **Functional uptakeities require binding-for-now.** They must be inhabited to work, but can remain revisable.

12. **Productive surprise is the update signal.** It increases the agent’s capacity for coherent continued adaptation under constraints.

13. **Visible seams are healthier than hidden mysteries.** The strongest settlements are those that can survive awareness of their own construction.
