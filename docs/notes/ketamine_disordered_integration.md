# Ketamine and Disordered Integration: A Working Note

*A sketch of a theoretical frame for what ketamine is doing to conscious experience, grounded in the cross-frequency coupling literature and anchored to specific phenomenological signatures. Not a finished theory — a working reference.*

## The core claim

Most of the existing literature on ketamine phenomenology tries to fit the experience into a REBUS-style "relaxed priors" frame inherited from the serotonergic psychedelic work. That frame captures something real but misses what seems to me to be the more specific and more interesting mechanism at play.

The claim, stated compactly:

**Ketamine doesn't primarily produce its characteristic phenomenology by loosening top-down priors. It produces it by disrupting the cross-frequency coupling that organizes temporal integration — specifically, the scaffolding that assembles the specious present and binds cross-modal signals into coherent subjective moments. The phenomenology is the experience of that integration machinery running with its gating and phase relationships scrambled.**

This is a narrower, more mechanistically specific claim than "ketamine relaxes priors," and it makes different predictions about what the phenomenology should look like — predictions that match the actual phenomenological record better than the precision-relaxation story does.

## Neuroscientific backdrop

The relevant chain of mechanism:

1. **Receptor level.** Subanesthetic ketamine blocks NMDA receptors, preferentially on fast-spiking parvalbumin-positive GABAergic interneurons. This disinhibits excitatory activity and changes the balance between NMDA-mediated (slow, integrative, recurrent) and AMPA-mediated (fast, feedforward) signaling. NMDA receptors are specifically the substrate for temporally-extended integration — they require coincident pre- and post-synaptic activity and have long decay constants, making them natural coincidence detectors across time.

2. **Oscillatory level.** The PV-interneuron disinhibition disrupts the generation and phase-structure of cortical oscillations, particularly gamma and theta, and — critically — the cross-frequency coupling between them. The empirical work here is pretty clear: ketamine alters theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling in dose-dependent ways, increases gamma and high-frequency oscillation power while disrupting the phase relationships that normally organize them, and produces aberrant cross-frequency coupling patterns across hippocampus, cortex, and striatum. The key references are Caixeta et al. (2013), Michaels's thesis work on chronic CFC effects, and the broader literature on NMDA antagonism and oscillatory dynamics.

3. **Computational level.** Theta serves as a temporal organizer — the slow clock that defines moments and orchestrates what gets bound within them. Gamma handles the fast binding of feature-coalitions into those moments. Theta-gamma phase-amplitude coupling is what gates which gamma-bound assemblies get admitted into which theta-defined window. This is the machinery that makes a coherent specious present: gamma binds features into assemblies, theta sequences those assemblies into successive moments, and the CFC between them determines what coheres with what.

4. **Phenomenological level.** The specious present — the felt ~100-300ms window of "now" that experience sits in — is the output of that integration process. When the CFC scaffolding is disrupted, the integration still runs (the system is still trying to bind), but the gating has broken down, so bindings happen that shouldn't, don't happen that should, and happen at the wrong phase relative to each other. The subject experiences the failure modes of the assembly process.

## The competing frame: REBUS-style relaxed priors

The dominant attempt to unify psychedelic and dissociative phenomenology through predictive processing is the Marguilho, Figueiredo, and Castro-Rodrigues (2023) unified model. They propose:

- Under low doses, ketamine disintegrates the salience network, relaxing priors about the embodied/bodily self → dissociative phenomenology
- Under high doses, ketamine disintegrates the default mode network, relaxing priors about narrative self → psychedelic phenomenology

This is a legitimate model and captures some of the phenomenology, especially the depersonalization/derealization aspects. But it has limitations:

- It's fundamentally about **which priors get relaxed**, not about **how temporal integration breaks down**. The model treats ketamine as a variant of the psychedelic precision-relaxation story, operating on different networks.
- It doesn't naturally predict the **specific temporal phenomenology** that's characteristic of ketamine — time loops, variable-speed time, fractal time, visible scene assembly. If ketamine is just relaxing different priors than LSD, why do the temporal signatures differ so dramatically?
- The Luppi et al. (2022) comparison of LSD, ketamine, and schizophrenia is suggestive here: all three show increased neural signal diversity, but ketamine and LSD show *reduced* front-to-back transfer entropy while schizophrenia shows *increased*. So the "entropy goes up" similarity obscures real structural differences in how integration is being disrupted. Ketamine and LSD aren't doing the same thing even if both increase some entropy metric.

## Phenomenological evidence

The key phenomenological features that the disordered-integration story predicts and that the relaxed-priors story doesn't handle as cleanly:

**Variable-speed time.** Experience of moments passing at variable rates, with stretches feeling compressed or dilated. Direct prediction of theta-period instability: if the slow clock that paces successive moments is no longer running at stable frequencies, the apparent duration of moments will vary. Not predicted by a generic prior-relaxation story.

**Time loops.** Experience of the same brief interval recurring, sometimes with subtle variation. Predicted by phase relationships losing their progressive structure — the theta clock entering patterns where similar phase relationships recur rather than advancing cleanly. The system is still organizing experience into moments, but the succession logic that normally moves from moment to next has broken down.

**Fractal time.** Experience of nested temporal scales becoming simultaneously apparent. If the normal subordination of fast oscillatory windows to slow ones is disrupted, the hierarchy of temporal windows could become experientially accessible rather than smoothed over. You experience the scaffolding instead of the scaffolding's product.

**Visible scene assembly.** Most famously, scenes constructing or changing with a visible left-to-right scan (or other spatial dynamic). If gamma-mediated binding into a theta window is slowed, the constituents of a "scene" may fail to bind simultaneously and instead be experienced as sequentially recruited. The spatial direction probably reflects the cortical dynamics of integration rather than being random. This is phenomenologically striking because it's direct experiential exposure to a process normally invisible — the binding of features into coherent scenes is something we normally experience only as its product, not as its process.

**Improper cross-modal binding.** Visual, proprioceptive, and vestibular signals getting bound together in ways that imply impossible body-states — acceleration, torque, squeezing of the body model — with the implied state directly apprehended. The crown jewel from a theory perspective, because it demonstrates two things at once:

1. The binding drive is intact (the system is still forming unified subjective moments); what's disrupted is the gating on which signals get bound together.
2. What we experience is the *output* of body-model inference, not its inputs. Garbled inputs produce coherent nonsense outputs, and we experience the nonsense outputs as directly as we normally experience correct ones. This is a very direct demonstration that ordinary bodily experience is model-output, not sensory-input.

**Frequency-specific auditory gain.** Selective amplification of particular frequency bands. A uniform precision-relaxation story predicts overall loosening; this predicts selective instability in the distribution of gain across the spectrum. Suggests the gating that normally distributes precision across frequencies has become erratic rather than uniformly turned down.

## Why this matters for theory of mind

The disordered-integration frame has philosophical implications beyond its mechanistic specificity.

**It collapses the distinction between the binding problem and the hard problem in an interesting way.** The standard philosophical treatment has these as separate: the binding problem is about how unified experience gets assembled from distributed neural activity (broadly empirical/architectural), while the hard problem is about why there's any experience at all (metaphysical). Ketamine phenomenology suggests they're more intimately related than the separate treatment suggests. When the binding machinery is disrupted, the phenomenology is simultaneously disrupted in ways that match the disruption. There isn't a separable "experience" that survives intact when binding breaks down, and then a separate question of "why is that experience there." What gets bound and whether there's a subjective moment at all appear to be two aspects of the same process.

**It's empirical pressure against zombie intuitions.** If you can perturb binding and have the phenomenology change in lockstep, the zombie thought experiment becomes harder to sustain. You're not just intuiting that binding could happen without phenomenology; you're being shown that specific structural features of binding and specific phenomenal features are coupled tightly enough that perturbing one perturbs the other in matching ways.

**It's evidence for a process view of experience.** The phenomenology under ketamine is not well-described as "consciousness with different content." It's better described as "the process of assembling consciousness, caught running abnormally." That only makes sense if consciousness is a process rather than a state — if there's an assembly happening all the time, normally smoothed over, that can become apparent when it runs slowly or unevenly. This fits the coalition/process view much better than it fits any view that treats consciousness as a state property of a unified subject.

**It gives a principled account of what altered states reveal.** The experiences aren't glimpses past the veil; they're the system's assembly process becoming phenomenally apparent because it's running slowly or wrong. This is a more defensible epistemic stance than either the credulous ("I saw the truth") or the dismissive ("your brain was broken, it means nothing") positions.

## Epistemic status and the warrant problem

The obvious problem: much of this frame has been shaped by first-person experience, and first-person reports from altered states are the weakest currency in this literature. The honest account of the epistemic structure:

**Phenomenology plays the role of data, not warrant.** The warrant for taking the disordered-integration story seriously is the CFC literature plus the computational/mechanistic argument — neither of which rests on any particular person's trip reports. The phenomenology is what the theory needs to predict in order to be any good; it's not what the theory is derived from.

**Personal experience plays the role of hypothesis-generation, not evidence.** The experiences are what made this connection salient to me, but the argument doesn't rest on them. There's a published phenomenological corpus (Jansen's *Ketamine: Dreams and Realities*, qualitative research in ketamine-assisted therapy, first-person reports at Erowid and in the qualitative literature) that any theoretical claim should be tested against.

**The analogy to lesion studies is the right frame.** We don't dismiss what stroke patients tell us about their experience because "their brain is perturbed." We take the systematic structure of their reports as evidence about the underlying architecture, precisely because the perturbation exposes structure that normal function smooths over. Pharmacological perturbations are the same kind of evidence, and the phenomenology is as legitimate a data source as behavioral deficits.

**The general epistemic stance.** Ordinary experience is already a construction, already subject to systematic distortions the subject can't detect from the inside (Gazzaniga's interpreter, change blindness, confabulation, choice blindness, memory reconstruction). There's no principled epistemic distinction between "sober" experience and "perturbed" experience that makes one uniquely trustworthy and the other uniquely untrustworthy. Both are outputs of the same machinery running in different regimes. The question is always the same: what does this phenomenology, produced under these conditions, tell us about the machinery that produced it?

## Specific falsifiable predictions

If the disordered-integration frame is right and the relaxed-priors frame is wrong (or only right at a different grain), the following should hold:

1. **Dose-response should track CFC disruption, not network disintegration.** Specifically, the phenomenological signatures I've described should correlate with theta-gamma PAC disruption metrics more tightly than with DMN/SN disintegration metrics. Testable with simultaneous EEG and structured phenomenological reporting during ketamine infusion.

2. **Other NMDA antagonists with different kinetics should produce systematically different temporal phenomenologies.** If it's NMDA antagonism per se that matters, compare memantine, PCP, DXM phenomenology — the temporal features should vary with the specific kinetic profile of each.

3. **Chronic users should show distinct phenomenological patterns from naive users.** Compensatory plasticity in response to chronic NMDA antagonism should alter the CFC profile in characteristic ways, and the phenomenology should shift correspondingly.

4. **Specific phenomenal features should map to specific CFC disruptions.** The visible scene-assembly should correlate with gamma coherence disruptions; the time loops should correlate with theta phase stability; the cross-modal binding failures should correlate with cross-regional coherence disruptions.

5. **The comparative phenomenology with classical psychedelics should be principled.** Psychedelic phenomenology should show preserved binding (form constants are tightly organized, narrative is wild but coherent) while ketamine should show disordered binding. Both relax high-level priors in some sense, but through different mechanisms with different computational signatures.

## What's missing / open questions

- The connection between the CFC literature and the phenomenological literature is largely unmade in the published work. The CFC people study rats; the PP people study humans. Nobody has seriously tried to bridge them with structured phenomenological reporting.

- The role of dissociative vs. psychedelic dose-regimes isn't well-handled by this frame yet. The Marguilho model's dose-dependent SN-vs-DMN story captures something, and the disordered-integration frame would need to account for why different dose ranges produce different phenomenological flavors.

- The mechanism connecting CFC disruption at the neural level to specific phenomenal features is hand-wavy. Saying "theta is the temporal organizer" and "gamma binds features" is true as far as it goes, but the inference from oscillatory dynamics to specific subjective features needs more careful work.

- The relationship to dissociation proper (not just the temporal/integration phenomena) — depersonalization, the "K-hole," out-of-body states — is underdeveloped in this frame. The Marguilho model handles these better; this frame needs to either absorb their account or give an alternative.
