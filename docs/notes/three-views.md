# Three Views of Mind, One Package

Philosophy of mind has a taxonomy problem. Computational functionalism, illusionism, and eliminativism are presented as competing answers to the question *what is the nature of mental states?* — and they do compete, in the sense that their partisans write papers against each other. But if you sit with the three positions long enough, they start to feel like dialects of the same language. The disagreements shrink as you press on them. The ontologies converge. What's left looks more like an argument about reference-fixing conventions than an argument about what exists.

I want to suggest that the feeling of collapse is mostly right, that the residue that remains is actually interesting, and that the interesting residue points to a claim about the architecture of self-modeling that doesn't belong to any of the three views on its own. Where exactly that architectural claim should be localized — in introspection specifically, in the broader process of narrative self-organization, or somewhere else — is a further question I won't try to settle here.

## The apparent collapse

Here are the three positions, stated charitably.

**Computational functionalism** says mental states are functional roles. A belief is whatever state is produced by certain inputs, produces certain outputs, and stands in certain relations to other mental states. The substrate doesn't matter; what matters is the pattern of causal relations. Silicon running the right program has beliefs just as much as a brain does.

**Illusionism** says that phenomenal consciousness — the "what it's like"-ness of experience, the felt intrinsicness of qualia — is a cognitive illusion. There genuinely seems to be ineffable, private, directly apprehended phenomenal character. But the seeming is produced by introspective mechanisms that misrepresent their own workings. The seeming is real and needs explaining; the qualia as traditionally conceived are not.

**Eliminativism**, in its classical Churchlandian form, says folk psychology is a bad proto-scientific theory. Beliefs, desires, pains — these are theoretical posits of a framework that mature neuroscience will replace, not vindicate. Like phlogiston, they'll be eliminated rather than reduced.

These sound like three different claims. But notice: all three agree on the physical and functional facts. None of them posits any entity the others deny. The functionalist and the illusionist agree that there are physical states which cause the agent to report, behave, and introspect as if it has beliefs and qualia. The eliminativist agrees too; the disagreement is about whether the folk terms successfully refer to those states. The functionalist says yes, reinterpreted. The illusionist says yes for beliefs but no for qualia-as-traditionally-conceived. The Churchlandian eliminativist says no across the board.

The ontology is shared. The fight is about semantics.

## Why the fight isn't nothing

A verbal dispute is still a dispute, and sometimes the verbal choice tracks something real. Here I think illusionism has a claim that isn't recoverable from functionalism alone, and eliminativism has a claim that's mostly wrong in the form its most famous proponents gave it.

Start with illusionism. The core architectural claim — that self-modeling systematically misrepresents its own objects — isn't just a gloss on functionalism. A vanilla functionalist can say introspection accurately reports functional states. The illusionist proposes something stronger: that some part of the system's self-modeling has a specific structural feature, which is that it represents its objects as having properties (intrinsicness, ineffability, privacy, direct acquaintance) that they don't actually have, with the misrepresentation running in a characteristic direction. Whether this distortion is best localized in introspection per se, or in a broader narrative process that compresses the system's internal multiplicity into something legible, is an open question. What matters for present purposes is that *some* such structural feature is being claimed — a claim about the architecture of a subsystem, which could in principle be true or false, and which isn't recoverable from semantic moves alone.

Now eliminativism. The Churchland move was to treat folk psychology as phlogiston — a false theory awaiting replacement. But phlogiston is doing a lot of work in that analogy, and the analogy doesn't generalize. Phlogiston was eliminated because it made wrong predictions and its successor theory explained everything phlogiston explained plus more, without needing phlogiston's ontology. Not every superseded framework has this shape.

Consider fluid dynamics. It isn't "real" in the sense that physics is real — there are no fluids at the level of fermions and bosons, no viscosity, no turbulence, no Reynolds number. These are all emergent properties of aggregates of particles. But no one thinks fluid dynamics should be eliminated. It's predictively indispensable, it carves real joints (laminar versus turbulent, compressible versus incompressible), and any successor theory at the particle level doesn't *replace* it — it *vindicates* it as a coarse-graining. Try designing an airplane wing from quantum chromodynamics and see how far you get.

The question for folk psychology, then, isn't "is it real at the fundamental level?" — nothing at the functional level is real in that sense. The question is "is it more like phlogiston or more like fluid dynamics?" Does it make wrong predictions awaiting a successor that explains everything it did plus more, or does it carve genuine functional joints that any successor theory will have to vindicate?

The Churchland bet was phlogiston. The functionalist bet is fluid dynamics. And folk psychology is too predictively successful to be phlogiston. We navigate social life by attributing beliefs and desires, and we are not hopelessly wrong. The attributions work. Neuroscience is not going to hand us a successor vocabulary in which "she believes it's raining" becomes unintelligible the way "it has phlogiston" is unintelligible now. It's going to hand us an implementation story.

So: eliminativism is wrong about the wholesale case but right about something narrower. Folk categories carry dualist contamination — a Cartesian residue that treats mental states as little inner items inspected by a homunculus. That residue does deserve elimination. What deserves vindication is the functional content. This is roughly Dennett's long-standing view, and it's the surgical version of eliminativism that's compatible with functionalism. The total Churchlandian version isn't.

## The move that matters

Here's the piece I think is underemphasized in the literature, and it's where the three views start looking less like rivals and more like components of a single package.

Illusionism is usually presented as a claim *added* to functionalism: the functionalist gives you the theory of mental states, and the illusionist comes along and explains why those states *seem* phenomenal when they aren't. Two layers.

But there is a case for thinking the seeming is closer to load-bearing than separable. For many of the states under discussion, seeming may be so tightly coupled to their functional role that treating it as an add-on gets the architecture backwards.

Consider: a belief that didn't *feel* real to its holder wouldn't function as a belief. A pain that didn't present as intrinsically awful wouldn't function as a pain. A desire that didn't show up as a felt pull toward its object wouldn't move behavior the way desires move behavior. The phenomenal seeming looks less like an epiphenomenal side-effect of the functional state and more like part of what makes the state play the role it plays.

If that's right, the misrepresentation isn't an unfortunate bug the system happens to have. It's doing work. The system represents its own states as phenomenally robust — as intrinsic, private, directly apprehended — and something about that representation appears to be involved in the functional work those states do.

I'll stop short of claiming the seeming is strictly constitutive of the functional role. That would foreclose a question I don't think this essay can settle: whether the coupling is architecturally necessary for any system playing these roles, or a local attractor selected for by the specific pressures our kind of system operates under. Either way, the coupling is tighter than the standard two-layer picture suggests. The "illusion" in illusionism may not be a separable layer that functionalism needs supplementing with — it may be internal enough to the functional role that separating them stops being illuminating.

This has a consequence for the standard anti-functionalist intuition pumps. The zombie thought experiment trades on the idea that you could have all the functional states without any of the "what it's like." If the seeming-what-it's-like is tightly coupled to the functional role — whether constitutively or near-constitutively — zombies become harder to coherently imagine than they initially appear. You may not be able to subtract the phenomenal seeming from the functional state as cleanly as the thought experiment assumes.

## The package

Put the pieces together and you get one position, not three.

At the level of ontology, there are physical states that play functional roles. This is functionalism.

Those functional states are tightly coupled to self-representations that present the states as phenomenally intrinsic, ineffable, and private. This is illusionism, but read as a structural claim about what's going on when functional states play their roles rather than as an additional layer.

The folk vocabulary we use to talk about these states is partly accurate — it tracks real functional joints — and partly contaminated by a Cartesian residue that should be eliminated. This is targeted eliminativism, not the wholesale kind.

Three claims, mutually compatible, each doing different work. They look like competitors because they've been presented as answers to the same question — *what's the right view in philosophy of mind?* — but they may actually be answers to three different questions that got bundled together by historical accident. What are mental states? (Functional roles.) What is the architecture of self-modeling? (Some form of systematic misrepresentation, running in a characteristic direction, located somewhere in the system's way of rendering its own states.) How should we interpret folk mental vocabulary? (Vindicate the functional content, eliminate the dualist residue.)

The sense of semantic collapse people have when comparing these views isn't a failure to see real distinctions. It's an accurate detection that the distinctions are playing different games. Once you separate the games, the positions stop competing and start composing.

Dennett has been in the neighborhood of this view for decades, and most of the parts are his. What I think is worth isolating is the observation that the seeming-phenomenal and the functional role are harder to prize apart than the standard two-layer picture suggests — that illusionism and functionalism, pressed carefully, may be describing the same architecture from different angles rather than contributing separable theoretical commitments.

One tempting next step would be to locate the relevant distortion in introspection itself — to say that introspection is where the misrepresentation happens and leave it there. But it may be better understood as arising in a broader process that compresses and stabilizes a system's shifting internal multiplicity into something narratively and behaviorally legible. On that picture, introspection would be one output channel among several, and the characteristic distortions would be features of the compression rather than of introspection per se. I bracket that larger question here. My more limited claim is that the three views are often treated as competitors only because they're taken to answer a single bundled question — and once the question is unbundled, what's left is more a composition than a rivalry.

One package, three jobs. The taxonomy confused us because it treated tools as rivals.
