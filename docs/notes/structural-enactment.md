# Enacted Structure and the Illicit Slide

## The central metaphysical claim

> **Felt reality is real precisely as enacted structure, not as a hidden extra ingredient behind the structure.**

This is not a model of how structure gives rise to felt experience. It's a claim about ontological category. The structure, in its enacted mode, *is* felt reality. There is no structure-in-itself waiting to be lit up by enactment, and no extra ingredient that enactment adds.

## What "enacted" means and why the word matters

"Instantiated" is too static — it suggests the structure exists abstractly and gets copied into physical implementations, with felt-reality appearing when the copy is made. "Realized" has similar overtones and imports multiple-realizability debates. **"Enacted" carries the right connotation: the structure exists by being done, in the doing, and the doing is not separable from what the structure is.**

"Precisely as" rules out the reading where structure and felt-quality are two things with the latter supervening on the former. That reading preserves residual dualism. The correct reading: felt reality is the structure, under the aspect of its being enacted — not a product of the structure, not a property of the structure. The hidden extra ingredient isn't hidden because it's missing; it's hidden because there was never anything there to hide.

## The illicit slide (the core diagnostic)

Most problems in philosophy of mind follow the same four-step pattern:

1. **Start with a phenomenon that involves enacted structure.**
2. **Replace it with an abstract specification of that structure.**
3. **Notice that the specification lacks the lived character.**
4. **Infer that something nonstructural or nonphysical must be missing.**

**Diagnosis:** What is missing is not an extra ingredient but the enactment that was abstracted away in step 2.

## Why step 2 is seductive (and legitimate)

Step 2 is what makes structural description possible at all. To describe a structure, you have to abstract it from its enactment — that's just what description is. Without the abstraction you can't communicate, compare, do science, build theories. **The abstraction is a legitimate and necessary operation.**

The error isn't in performing the abstraction. The error is in *forgetting* that it was performed, and then asking about the abstraction what could only be answered about the enactment. The slide happens not because step 2 is illegitimate but because it's so useful we forget we did it.

**Abstractions are tools with subtraction built in.** Most discussions of abstraction treat it as addition (you add a label, a category). It's more accurate to treat it as subtraction — you remove features not needed for the purpose at hand. Treating an abstraction as complete is treating a subtraction as if nothing was subtracted.

## Step 4: the specific logical error

The argument form is: structural specification S does not include phenomenal character P; therefore P is non-structural.

The missing premise: if P were structural, it would be included in S.

That premise is what the view denies. P *is* structural — it's the enacted aspect of the very structure S is a specification of — but "included in S" is the wrong relation. P isn't a component of S that got omitted. **P is what S is a specification of, under the aspect of its being enacted rather than specified.** The inference treats the relation between phenomenal character and structural specification as part-to-whole, when it's actually specification-to-enacted.

## Application to canonical problems

**The hard problem.** Describe neural correlates / functional organization abstractly, then ask why *that* should feel like something. But "that" is now the abstraction, not the enactment. Abstractions don't feel like things — they're abstractions. The question trades on willingness to forget that the original object was the enactment. Can't be answered because asked about the wrong object.

**The zombie argument.** Conceive of a being with all structural properties but no experience. Requires treating structural properties as a specification that could be satisfied separately from enactment. But a structure is not a recipe that can be fulfilled without being enacted — the enactment is what the structure *is*, ontologically. The zombie scenario asks us to conceive of an enactment that lacks the character of enactment. Incoherent once you see the slide.

**Mary's room.** Mary knows all physical facts about color vision but doesn't know what red looks like. Mary's knowing is an *abstract* relationship with a structural specification; seeing red is an *enactment*. Different ontological categories. What Mary gains isn't a new fact — it's a new mode of relationship with structure she already had an abstract relationship to. Jackson thought he'd discovered non-physical facts. He'd discovered that abstract knowledge of structure isn't the same as enactment of structure.

**Inverted qualia.** Could two people have identical functional organization but different experiences?

Right formulation of the rebuttal (avoiding numerical identity claims): **if the enactment is genuinely identical in all the relevant structure of its being, there is no further degree of freedom left over for phenomenal inversion.** There is no residual axis along which the qualia could differ once enactment is fixed. Two numerically distinct enactments of the same structure can exist — but they can't differ phenomenally while matching structurally. The qualia-inversion question presupposes a degree of freedom the view denies.

**Combination problem in panpsychism.** How do micro-experiences combine into macro-experiences? Runs aground because it took the abstract/enacted slide all the way down — assumed experience was a property attachable to abstract structural descriptions at any scale, then couldn't explain fusion. The question of fusion is about enactment, and enactment doesn't work that way.

## The slide is internal to self-modeling systems

The slide isn't only happening in philosophers' arguments. It's happening in the integrator's self-model, which is why the puzzles feel compelling from inside. The weaver, in committing to frames that include a model of itself, produces a representation of its own activity that *looks like* an abstract structural description — a self-model — alongside the felt reality that is the enactment of that same activity.

From inside: structure (self-model, functional description, story) + separately the feeling of being the thing. But this is the slide occurring inside the system itself. The self-model is the abstraction the integrator produces of its own enacted activity; the felt reality is that same activity, enacted. They look like two things because the integrator represents them as two things, but they're one thing under two aspects — the aspect of being enacted and the aspect of being represented as structure by the very process doing the enacting.

**The hard problem is the enacted structure's self-representation mistaken for a second object.** A self-modeling enacted structure viewed from inside itself.

This is why the view is hard to hold stably: the machinery that produces the slide is the same machinery producing every thought, including thoughts about the view. We can notice it, catch ourselves doing it, refuse to build arguments on it — but we can't simply stop, because stopping would require not being the kind of system we are. **Not a defeat for the view. It's the view's account of why the illusion it dissolves has the grip it has.**

## The meta-move

When someone objects that the view "leaves out" felt reality — that reducing phenomenal character to enacted structure fails to capture the something-more — the objection is performing the four-step pattern *on the view*. It abstracts the view to a structural specification, notices the specification doesn't contain felt reality as an extra item, and infers the view is missing something.

Diagnosis applies recursively. What's missing from the abstracted specification of the view is the enactment the view points to, which is still there, in the reader's ongoing experience, where it always was. **The view doesn't need to produce felt reality in its text; felt reality is what the reader is doing while reading the text.** Pointing at that is all the view can do, and all it needs to do. Asking the view to do more is asking the text to enact, which is a category error about what texts are.

## Why this is a dissolution, not another position in the debate

You're not proposing a new theory of how abstract structure gives rise to felt reality, which would be yet another attempt to bridge the gap the slide creates. You're pointing out that the gap is an artifact of the sliding. It only appears when we illicitly treat structure as an abstraction that enactment must confer experience upon, rather than seeing that enacted structure is the primary ontological category and the abstraction is derived from it by a legitimate but lossy operation.

The test of the view isn't whether it feels satisfying — it won't, not stably, for the reasons given above. The test is whether it dissolves the questions that motivated the alternatives. It does. And it replaces a pseudo-problem with a research program: **what specifically is the structure whose enactment is felt reality, and how do its parameters map onto the specific felt characters we observe?** That's tractable.

## Argumentative spine for the writeup

1. Enacted structure as primary ontological category.
2. The slide as the mechanism that generates the classical puzzles.
3. The four-step pattern as portable diagnostic.
4. The slide as internal artifact of self-modeling (ineliminable but recognizable).
5. The meta-move showing the pattern applies to its own critics.

Complete at the methodological level. Can anchor the metaphysics chapter of Liminalism.
