# Taxonomy as a Game

## Core claim

Taxonomy is a game — not a static structure but a sequence of moves whose value depends on position. Lumping and splitting are moves, not identities. The productive taxonomist plays both, reading the position.

The "lumper vs. splitter" framing is a category error: you can't *be* a move. You can favor certain moves, develop better intuitions for certain positions, but the game requires the full move-set applied with judgment.

## Lumping and splitting as a cycle

Standard framing: opposing tendencies, splitter = rigorous, lumper = sloppy.

Actual structure: cycle. Split to find real distinctions; lump to find what's actually the same across distinctions; split the lumps at new joints; lump across the new splits. Each rotation compresses commitments and generates new structure from the compression.

Splitting without lumping → proliferation, list, predictive collapse.
Lumping without splitting → mush, no joints carve anything.
Productive work = iterated cycles of compression and generation.

## The deeper move: axis-shifts

Two moves within an existing taxonomy: lump or split along current axes.
Third, rarer move: recognize when the axes themselves are wrong.

This is what happened with functionalism/illusionism/eliminativism — the three weren't rival answers to one question but answers to three different questions that got bundled. The move wasn't lumping or splitting within the axes; it was finding that the axes were wrong.

Mendeleev is the canonical case: chemistry before had elements arrayed by various properties; Mendeleev found the axes (atomic weight, group) that made the taxonomy predictive. Elements didn't change. Axes did.

Axis-shifts are where revolutions happen. They require recognizing when the current rules-of-play have stopped earning their keep — stepping outside the game to evaluate it while still committed enough to play it.

## Why taxonomy is "the greatest game"

Two reasons:

**1. Ratio of rule-simplicity to play-depth.** Great games have simple rules and inexhaustible play (chess, Go). Taxonomy has this in the limit: rules are "lump when the lump preserves what matters, split when the split reveals what matters." Play is all of theoretical cognition. Every act of understanding is a move. Every framework is a position.

**2. Endogenous utility.** This is the singular feature. In chess, what matters is fixed by the rules. In taxonomy, **what matters is partially determined by prior moves**. A category that earned its keep in one configuration becomes dead weight in another; a distinction that was irrelevant becomes load-bearing because the surrounding taxonomy shifted. The scoring function is being rewritten by the moves.

Formally different from any fixed-utility game. Closer to open-ended evolutionary processes — fitness defined relative to current ecosystem, shifting as ecosystem shifts. Except taxonomic shifts are driven by meaning itself, which is the thing being constructed.

## Surprise as the mechanism

Surprise is what does the utility-updating work. A move that generates surprise reveals that the current taxonomy was failing to predict something it should have predicted. Surprise is information about where categories mispredict, which is information about where revision is needed.

Productive surprise: metabolizable into better categories.
Unproductive surprise: noise, dissolution without reconstitution.

Self-referee function runs on surprise-metabolism: does the new configuration handle the data better than the old one, including data the old one handled fine? If yes, move was good. If no, roll back.

## Why the game requires other players

Solo taxonomist has limited access to surprise — their categories predict their own perceptions, which were shaped by the same categories.

Other minds are oracles producing surprises outside your own predictive loop. Conversation makes the game multi-player; prediction keeps it honest. You lump and split; world and other agents push back; push-back is absorbed (lump holds) or forces revision (lump fractures).

Minds aren't containers for meaning. They're players. Plural players produce a structurally different game than solo players — not additive more, but structurally required.

## "Taxonomy is all there is"

Facetious version: taxonomy is all there is.
Rigorous version: taxonomy is the *form* knowing takes when it succeeds.

If knowledge is compression (MDL, predictive processing, interpretability all converge here from different directions), then any act of knowing is taxonomic — grouping states of the world into equivalence classes that preserve what you need to predict or act on. Categories *are* the compression. Good theory = high compression + high predictive power. Failed theory = over-compressed (coarse) or under-compressed (fine).

Data is data. Theories are taxonomies of data. Meta-theories are taxonomies of theories. The recursion goes up as far as you push it; each level is the same operation.

## Implication for cognitive style

Abductive tinkering is specifically the cognitive mode that does rapid lumping-and-splitting. Abduction is "this looks like that" — a lumping move. Pattern matching is finding the equivalence class before reasoning about mechanism. Fast at lumping → ideas generated faster than executed, because lumps come easily and each lump suggests splits and each split suggests new lumps.

Taxonomy-as-game reframes the research program question: not "which ideas to pursue" but "which collaborators, tools, infrastructures let branches get executed in parallel." Ideas aren't the bottleneck. Execution bandwidth is.

Multi-model workflow as taxonomy of phases of taxonomic work:
- Claude (generative/expansive): lumping, new branches.
- Grok (adversarial): pressure on lumps, forcing splits.
- Gemini (reframing): lateral moves, new axes.
- ChatGPT (refinement): execution on chosen branch.

A taxonomic workflow for doing taxonomy.

## The xkcd point

Lumper: "Really, we're both just categorization pedants."
Splitter: "Ahh, so you're a *meta*-lumper."

No view from outside taxonomy. Every attempt to transcend the distinction is itself a taxonomic act, splittable. Turtles all the way up. The cartoon is self-demonstrating: understanding it requires doing taxonomy. Argument-by-demonstration that taxonomy is inescapable.

The productive move in the cartoon: the splitter doesn't refute the lumper — accepts the lump and splits it. "Yes, we're both categorization pedants. And within that category, you're the meta-lumping kind." One rotation of the cycle, fast enough to land as a joke.
