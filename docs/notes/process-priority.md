# Process-Priority and Taxonomy-as-Game

## The methodological stance

Process-priority is **anti-reification without being anti-realism.** It does not say:

- objects are fake
- abstractions are illegitimate
- stability is an illusion

It says:

- **stability is often achieved**
- **objects are often process-made**
- **abstractions are tools with subtraction built into them**
- **ontology goes wrong when we forget the generative history of the things we now treat as basic**

It's not a claim that everything is ontologically processual. It's a claim that many of the reified things we take as foundational are actually stable, useful, sometimes indispensable outputs of processes — and that inquiry goes wrong when we forget this.

## Why the distinction from radical process metaphysics matters

Radical process views ("everything is flux, objects are illusions") overshoot into anti-realism and then have to explain why the world appears so stable, why object-talk works, why science succeeds by treating things as having enduring properties. Explanations end up handwavy or covertly smuggle objects back through the back door.

Ordinary object-ontology handles stability fine but can't handle the dynamics that produce it, and gets puzzled whenever stability breaks down or the "same" object undergoes radical change.

**Process-priority takes both seriously.** Stability is real and often achieved; the achievement is what needs accounting for.

## Modes of stability (an important refinement)

Not all stability is the same kind:

- **Inertial stability** — a rock persists because disrupting forces aren't present
- **Feedback stability** — a thermostat corrects deviations
- **Equilibrium stability** — a market clears through aggregate transactions
- **Ongoing-activity stability** — the weaver maintains coherent experience through continuous activity

All real. All count as stability. But *how* stability is maintained determines what would disrupt it, what range of conditions it holds under, and its relationship to underlying dynamics.

**The reification error treats all stabilities as if they were the rock kind.** Mental stability is almost entirely of the ongoing-activity kind: identity by coherence-work, memory by entanglement-work, experience by weaving. Treating these as inertial gets the kind of stability wrong, and the questions you then ask are miscalibrated.

You ask "what is the self made of" when you should ask "what activity sustains the coherent appearance of a self." You ask "where are memories stored" when you should ask "what processes produce reliable re-enactment of past commitments." The questions aren't senseless but locally wrong, and the wrongness traces to the reification step.

## Synthesis with taxonomy-as-game

Taxonomy-as-game already establishes:

- categorization choices are endogenously driven by what matters for the purpose at hand
- lumping and splitting are moves rather than discoveries
- the "right" taxonomy organizes inquiry productively given current goals

**Process-priority is the ontological variation of the game.** Reification is lumping (subsuming a process-history under a stable label). De-reification is splitting (unfolding the label back into its generative process). When to do which is governed by the same considerations that govern any categorization choice.

Process-priority isn't a separate doctrine requiring independent argument. It's the application of taxonomy-as-game to the case where the categorizations are **ontological rather than merely classificatory**.

## The surprise criterion

When is a reification working, and when does it need reopening? Surprise is the signal.

**A reified output is doing good work when** it organizes inquiry, reduces surprise productively, and supports stable inference and action.

**A reified output needs to be reopened when it starts generating the wrong kind of surprise:**

- sterile paradox
- pseudo-problems
- explanatory blockage
- intuitions that feel deep only because a subtraction has been forgotten
- sharp contradictions between what the tool says and what the generative process would predict

**General principle:** Reification is justified until it starts surprising us in ways that indicate it has hidden too much of its own generative history.

## Why surprise is the right criterion (not logical contradiction or empirical falsification)

- **Logical contradiction is too strict.** Many reifications produce conclusions that are internally consistent but still wrong (the zombie argument is consistent; it just operates on the wrong object).
- **Empirical falsification is too permissive.** Many reifications aren't empirically testable directly.
- **Surprise catches cases where the system containing the reification is giving the observer signals that something is off**, without requiring the off-ness to be formalized or measured.

Consistent with the active-inference vocabulary the framework uses throughout: surprise is prediction error; prediction error drives updating at every level. Using surprise as the criterion for when to de-reify is just applying the same signal-processing the framework uses everywhere else.

## Diagnostic signals in detail

**Sterile paradox** (e.g. zombies): abstraction asked to handle cases its subtraction can't accommodate, producing conclusions formally derivable but tracking nothing.

**Pseudo-problems** (e.g. the hard problem): questions whose formulation depends on a reification that, once opened, dissolves the question.

**Explanatory blockage**: the reification prevents seeing the dynamics that would answer your question; you hit a wall because the wall *is* the reified category.

**Intuitions that feel deep only because a subtraction has been forgotten**: the subtlest and most important. Catches cases where reification produces confident certainty about an artifact. *The confidence itself is the signal* — if an intuition feels unshakeable but tracks something a process account would dissolve, the unshakeable feeling is the reification's signature.

**Sharp contradictions between the tool and its origin**: the reification and its generative process giving different answers. Cleanest case, because it's a logical signal that something was lost in abstraction.

## Why the hard problem specifically persists

It produces non-productively sterile surprise — doesn't point anywhere, doesn't generate new predictions, doesn't refine over time. It reproduces itself in new vocabularies (Cartesian → functionalist → physicalist → emergentist → panpsychist → illusionist → IIT) without changing. A productive puzzle sharpens as you work on it; a sterile puzzle reproduces itself in new vocabularies without changing.

**That's diagnostic.** The sterility of the surprise was itself a signal that something needed reopening at a deeper level than any of those frameworks could reach from within themselves.

The enacted/abstract slide is what they all share. Which is what the surprise was pointing at all along.

## Unified principle across the framework

The same epistemic dynamic operates at every level: **commit to a stable representation until surprise forces reopening.**

- **Fastest timescale:** integrator weaves arc-candidates, keeps some provisional, commits as entanglement accumulates
- **Intermediate timescale:** metric updates under sufficient error signal
- **Slow timescale:** scientific/philosophical concepts held until they produce sterile paradox

Same algorithmic shape governs moment-to-moment experience, lifetime identity formation, and the progress of inquiry. Not coincidence — coherent self-maintaining systems at every level face the same trade-off: stability buys efficiency and predictability, but rigidity produces failures of fit, and surprise is the signal telling the system when the trade-off needs renegotiating.

## Connection to Whitehead and process philosophy

Whitehead had the metaphysical instinct but couldn't operationalize it. Earlier process philosophers failed to gain traction not because the move was wrong but because they didn't apply it with the right specificity and didn't have the computational vocabulary to say what the processes actually are.

The current framework has both. It gives specific, cashable accounts of what the processes are (coherence-maintenance, arc-commitment, metric-deformation, coalition-coordination) and what their outputs are (committed frames, stabilized memories, identity, felt reality). The intervening century produced the conceptual tools — dynamical systems, active inference, machine learning, network science — that let process-priority be operationalized rather than just gestured at.

## Why it's a humble metaphysical position

Process-priority doesn't claim to have figured out the deep structure of reality. It locates the error in **us**, not in the world. Things we treat as basic aren't wrong to exist — we're wrong to forget how they came to exist when that matters for what we're doing with them. The world contains processes and their stable outputs; the error is in our handling of the relationship between them.

This is a humble metaphysical position but a substantive epistemological and methodological one. Disciplined diagnosis of how inquiry goes wrong, and what to do about it, is what's needed in the current moment. Grand metaphysical systems are out of fashion for good reason.

## The framework's recursive coherence

Every major move in Liminalism is the same methodological commitment applied to a different reified category:

- enacted-structure dissolution
- weaver as process rather than narrator
- metric as regularity rather than object
- coalition as differentiated activity rather than assembled parts
- self as Nash equilibrium rather than substance
- memory as entanglement-pattern rather than record
- the past as the current coalition's stabilized trajectory rather than a record of what happened

**The framework isn't a bunch of separate theses that cohere. It's one methodological stance, systematically applied, producing theses that cohere because they're all doing the same work.**

And the framework's recursion: the general methodological commitment is itself a stable output of the process of thinking through specific cases, not a foundational starting point imposed from outside. The stance arose by its own lights — by surprise forcing the reopening of prior reifications. Which is how such a stance should arise, if it's correct.
